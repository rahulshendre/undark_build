# Arbitration (loan agreement clause)

## When used
When the loan agreement contains an **arbitration clause**, disputes may be
referred to a private arbitrator instead of court; the award is then enforceable
like a civil decree.

## Who files / initiates
Either party can invoke the clause; in recovery, the lender (via authorized
representative) usually initiates per the agreement's procedure.

## Eligibility
- A valid **arbitration agreement/clause** in the loan contract.
- The dispute falls within the clause's scope and is arbitrable.

## Required documents
- The loan agreement containing the arbitration clause
- Statement of account / computation of dues
- Notice invoking arbitration
- KYC and authorization

Intended to be quicker than litigation; depends on the appointed arbitrator and

## Limitations
- Needs a pre-existing clause — no clause, no arbitration.
- Cost of the arbitrator can be disproportionate for very small tickets.

## Typical use cases
- NBFC/fintech loan agreements that include an arbitration clause.
- Where the lender prefers a private, contract-based route to a decree-equivalent award.

## Decision tree
```
Does the loan agreement contain an arbitration clause?
  ├─ no  → arbitration not available → Lok Adalat / NI Act / Order 37 / civil suit
  └─ yes → is the cost proportionate to a sub-₹1 lakh claim?
       ├─ yes → invoke arbitration → award → enforce
       └─ no  → consider Lok Adalat / NI Act instead for cost reasons
```

## Common mistakes
- Invoking arbitration without checking the clause's exact procedure (seat, appointment, notice).
- Ignoring that the **award still needs enforcement** to recover money.
- Using costly arbitration for a tiny claim where Lok Adalat would be cheaper and final.
