# Order XXXVII CPC — Summary Suit

## When used
To recover a **liquidated** (fixed, ascertained) sum based on a negotiable
instrument (bill of exchange, hundi, promissory note) or a written contract,
faster than an ordinary suit — because the defendant cannot defend as of right.

## Who files
The creditor (lender, through its authorized representative/practitioner) in the
appropriate civil court.

## Eligibility
- The claim is for a **fixed sum** (debt/liquidated demand), not damages.
- It arises from a negotiable instrument or a written contract/enactment.
- The debt is clearly evidenced in writing.

## Required documents
- The promissory note / cheque / written loan contract
- Statement of account and computation of the fixed sum
- Demand notice + proof of dispatch
- KYC and authorization

## Expected timeline
Faster than an ordinary suit because the defendant must obtain **leave to
defend** (and may be required to deposit/secure the amount as a condition). If
leave is refused or not sought in time, the plaintiff can move for judgment.

## Limitations
- Only for liquidated sums on the qualifying instruments/contracts — not for unclear or disputed-quantum claims.
- If the court grants unconditional leave to defend, the speed advantage is largely lost and it proceeds like a regular suit.

## Typical use cases
- Debt evidenced by a **promissory note** or signed loan agreement with a clear sum.
- Where a cheque exists but the practitioner wants a civil **money decree** (often run alongside or instead of NI Act §138).

## Decision tree
```
Is the debt a fixed sum on a PN / cheque / written contract?
  ├─ no  → ordinary civil suit
  └─ yes → file Order 37 summary suit
            └─ defendant seeks leave to defend?
                 ├─ refused / conditional → fast path to decree
                 └─ unconditional leave   → proceeds like a normal suit
```

## Common mistakes
- Using Order 37 for an unliquidated/disputed-quantum claim (gets converted/dismissed).
- Weak written evidence of the debt (defeats the summary advantage).
- Not pressing the leave-to-defend stage where conditions (deposit/security) could be sought.
